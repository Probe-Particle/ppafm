The total site-energy shift of Molecule $A$ due to the presence of Molecule $B$ is the sum of four terms:

$$ \Delta \omega_{total} = \Delta \omega_{Coulomb} + \Delta \omega_{Pol} + \Delta \omega_{Exch} + \Delta \omega_{CT} $$

Here are the exact mathematical formulas for the three new terms, how to compute them using the data you just exported, and the seminal references that established them.

---

### 1. Mutual Polarization (Dielectric Induction Shift)
**Physics:** When Molecule A is excited, its electron density changes ($\Delta \rho_A$). This creates a change in the electric field ($\Delta \mathbf{F}$) at the site of Molecule B. Molecule B's electron cloud distorts (polarized by $\boldsymbol{\alpha}_B$), creating a reaction field that reaches back and lowers the energy of Molecule A's excited state.

**The Mathematical Formula:**
Derived from classical electrodynamics for polarizable media:
$$ \Delta \omega_{Pol} = - \mathbf{F}_B^{(A_0)} \cdot \boldsymbol{\alpha}_B \cdot \Delta \mathbf{F}_B - \frac{1}{2} \Delta \mathbf{F}_B \cdot \boldsymbol{\alpha}_B \cdot \Delta \mathbf{F}_B $$

*   $\boldsymbol{\alpha}_B$: The $3 \times 3$ polarizability tensor of Molecule B (from your `polarizability_tensor.txt`).
*   $\mathbf{F}_B^{(A_0)}$: The ground-state electric field generated by A at the center of B.
*   $\Delta \mathbf{F}_B$: The *difference* in the electric field caused by the excitation ($\Delta \mathbf{F}_B = \mathbf{F}_B^{(A^*)} - \mathbf{F}_B^{(A_0)}$). 

*How to calculate it from your data:*
You can easily calculate $\Delta \mathbf{F}_B$ by taking the numerical gradient (spatial derivative) of the potential generated by your `density_difference_state1.cube` at the coordinate center of Molecule B.

> **Reference:** A. J. Stone, *The Theory of Intermolecular Forces*, Oxford University Press, 2013. (Chapter 5: Induction Energy).

---

### 2. Pauli Exchange-Repulsion (Steric Shift)
**Physics:** Electrons cannot occupy the same space. When Molecule A is excited, the electron jumps to the LUMO. The LUMO is physically larger and more diffuse than the HOMO. Therefore, the excited molecule "bumps into" Molecule B more than the ground-state molecule did. This causes a **repulsive (positive/blue) shift** in the excitation energy.

**The Mathematical Formula:**
Calculating exact Pauli repulsion requires massive multi-electron antisymmetrization operators. However, in modern computational physical chemistry, we use the highly accurate **Density Overlap Model**. Pauli repulsion is directly proportional to the spatial overlap of the electron densities.

$$ \Delta \omega_{Exch} = K_{exch} \int \Delta \rho_A(\mathbf{r}) \cdot \rho_B^{GS}(\mathbf{r}) \, d\mathbf{r} $$

*   $\Delta \rho_A(\mathbf{r})$: Your `density_difference_state1.cube` (Molecule A).
*   $\rho_B^{GS}(\mathbf{r})$: Your `ground_state_density.cube` (Molecule B).
*   $K_{exch}$: A scaling constant for the kinetic energy of the overlapping electrons (typically fitted, often around $5-10$ eV·Bohr$^3$).

*How to calculate it from your data:*
This is incredibly easy for you to implement! It is exactly the same code as your Coulomb shift, but instead of multiplying $\Delta \rho_A$ by the *potential* of B, you multiply it by the *electron density* of B and sum over the grid: `np.sum(cube_diff * cube_gs) * dV`.

> **Reference:** S. Amovilli, B. Mennucci, *"Density Overlap as a Measure of Pauli Repulsion"*, **J. Phys. Chem. A** 1997, 101, 14, 2616–2622.

---

### 3. Charge-Transfer Mixing (Superexchange Red Shift)
**Physics:** This is the most important term for symmetric molecules like PTCDA. Because PTCDA has no permanent dipole, the Coulomb shift is very small. The massive optical shifts seen in PTCDA crystals actually come from **quantum mechanical mixing with Charge Transfer (CT) states**.
Even if the electron doesn't permanently leave Molecule A, the *virtual possibility* of hopping to Molecule B (forming $A^+ B^-$) stabilizes the Frenkel exciton through second-order perturbation theory.

**The Mathematical Formula:**
$$ \Delta \omega_{CT} \approx - \frac{|t_e|^2}{E_{CT} - E_{S1}} - \frac{|t_h|^2}{E_{CT} - E_{S1}} $$

*   $E_{S1}$: The isolated excitation energy (from your `aggregate_parameters.json`).
*   $E_{CT}$: The energy of the Charge Transfer state (Approx: $IP_A - EA_B - V_{coulomb}$).
*   $t_e$: The **Electron Transfer Integral** (electronic coupling between LUMO of A and LUMO of B).
*   $t_h$: The **Hole Transfer Integral** (electronic coupling between HOMO of A and HOMO of B).

*How to calculate $t_e$ and $t_h$ from your data:*
This uses the `qm_matrices.npz` (MO Coefficients $C$ and Overlap matrix $S$) you just exported. 
The transfer integral is the Fock matrix element between the localized orbitals of A and B:
$$ t_{e} \approx \langle \text{LUMO}_A | \hat{F} | \text{LUMO}_B \rangle $$
*(Note: To compute this rigorously without writing a complex Fock-builder yourself, this is the exact step where you feed your `.chk` file into a tool like **VOTCA** or the **dipro** plugin, which automates this matrix projection).*

> **Reference:** F. C. Spano, *"Nature of Singlet Excitons in Oligoacene Solid Films"*, **Accounts of Chemical Research** 2010, 43, 3, 429–439. (This paper specifically proves why $\Delta \omega_{CT}$ dominates in molecules like yours).
